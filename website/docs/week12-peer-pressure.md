# Week 12: The Alignment Problem in Groups
*Game Theory in Groups*

You have spent two units learning to read your own system and three weeks learning to read the systems between you and other people. Now we look at the third level: groups.

A group is not just a collection of individuals. It is a system with its own goals — sometimes spoken, often unspoken. When a group's goals match your own, things feel easy and natural. When they diverge, you face an **alignment problem**: do you update your goals to match the group, or do you hold your ground?

This is the engineering core of peer pressure. It is not a moral failure to feel pulled toward what a group wants. The pull is a real, predictable feature of how humans are wired. We are social animals. Our hardware was built for group survival. Feeling the pull is normal.

The skill is **recognizing** the alignment problem in real time, naming it, and choosing — deliberately — whether to update or hold.

---

:::info Facilitator Snapshot
- The big idea: peer pressure is an alignment problem, not a character test.
- The skill: noticing when the group's goal differs from yours, and deciding deliberately.
- Don't frame this as "say no to peer pressure." Frame it as "see the alignment problem clearly."
- This week starts Unit 4 — game theory in groups. Includes partner activities.

## Facilitator Preparation

:::info Before You Begin
- Have the Telemetry Log accessible.
- Think about a recent alignment problem you've faced as an adult — a moment where the group wanted one thing and you wanted another. Sharing your real example helps a lot.
- For Session 2, you'll do a role-play. If you don't have a partner for the student, the facilitator can play the group.
:::

:::tip Facilitation Mindset
The classic anti-peer-pressure curricula treat this as a binary: "say no." Real life is much more interesting. Sometimes the group is right — updating your goal IS the smart move. Sometimes the group is wrong and you need to hold. Sometimes the group isn't even fully aware of its goal until you name it out loud.

The goal of this week is to give the student a vocabulary that handles all those cases — not a slogan.
:::

## For Younger Learners (Ages 8–9)

:::info Adapting This Week
**Simplest version of the concept:** "Sometimes the group wants one thing and you want a different thing. That's normal. The skill is noticing it and deciding what to do."

**What to shorten or skip:**
- Skip "alignment problem" language. Use "the group wants ___ and I want ___."
- Skip the more nuanced "update" scenarios. Focus on holding when the group is doing something that doesn't match the student's values.

**Adapting the activities:**
- Use stories from books or shows. Identify scenes where a character faced a group goal vs. their own goal.
- Use simple kid examples: everyone wants to play tag but you want to read; everyone is laughing at someone and you don't think it's funny.

**Journal alternative:** "A time when the group wanted ___ and I wanted ___ was ___. I decided to ___."

**What success looks like:** The student can identify one situation from their life where the group wanted something different than them — and describe what they did or wish they had done.
:::

## Guided Session 1
### Spotting the Alignment Problem
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- describe an alignment problem as a mismatch between the group's goal and the individual's goal
- identify the body signals that show up when they're in an alignment problem
- name three common alignment problems from their own life

---

#### Activities

**1. The Group Goal**

Explain:

> "Every group has goals. Some are obvious — a soccer team's goal is to win the game. Some are unspoken — a friend group's goal might be 'have fun together' or 'avoid awkwardness' or 'not let anyone be left out.' Most group goals are mixed: some spoken, some not.
>
> When the group's goal matches what YOU want, things feel easy. You don't have to decide anything — you just go with the flow. When the group's goal *doesn't* match what you want, that's an **alignment problem**. The system is asking you to do one thing while you want another."

---

**2. The Body Signal of an Alignment Problem**

Alignment problems usually show up in the body before the brain knows what's happening:

- A slight tightening in the stomach
- A pulled-back feeling — like you're holding your body still
- A laugh that doesn't quite feel real
- A "yes" coming out of your mouth that feels like it belongs to someone else
- A sudden urge to go to the bathroom or check your phone

These are not symptoms of weakness. They are the **telemetry of an alignment problem**. Your system is telling you: "Hey — there's a goal mismatch happening right now."

Most kids ignore this telemetry. The skill of this week is learning to *notice* it.

---

**3. Real-Life Alignment Problems**

Together, list three alignment problems the student has actually faced. Some categories:

**Everyday alignment:**
- Group wants to do one activity, you want to do another
- Group is going somewhere, you're tired and want to go home
- Group is making fun of someone, you don't think it's funny

**Higher-stakes alignment:**
- Group wants to break a rule you don't want to break
- Group is excluding someone and you don't want to participate
- Group is asking you to do something that violates a real value

Notice: alignment problems range from low-stakes (whose house to play at) to high-stakes (whether to participate in something that feels wrong). The skill is the same in all of them — *notice the mismatch, then decide deliberately*.

## Guided Session 2
### Update or Hold?
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- describe the two options when facing an alignment problem: **update** (change your goal to match the group) or **hold** (keep your own goal)
- name three questions to ask before deciding which one
- practice holding ground in a low-stakes role-play

---

#### Activities

**1. Two Real Options**

When you face an alignment problem, you have two real options:

- **Update:** Change your goal to match the group. ("I don't really care that much, and the group cares more — I'll go along.")
- **Hold:** Keep your own goal. ("I care about this enough that I'm not going to change just because the group wants something else.")

Both are valid. Updating isn't weak. Holding isn't selfish. The point is to *decide* — not to drift.

The third option — the one that causes most regret — is to **drift**: not actually deciding, just going along while feeling bad about it. Drifting is the option this week is trying to prevent.

---

**2. The Three Questions**

Before deciding, ask yourself:

**Q1: How much do I care about my goal?**
(0 = I don't really care, 10 = this is core to who I am)

**Q2: Is the group's goal harmful to anyone — including me?**
(If yes, hold. Always.)

**Q3: Would I be okay with this decision tomorrow morning?**
(Future-you is a great consultant. If future-you would regret it, that's a sign.)

If you score Q1 below 5, Q2 is "no," and Q3 is "yes" — updating is probably fine. If any of those tip the other way, you probably want to hold.

---

**3. Role-Play: Holding Ground**

Role-play one alignment problem. The facilitator plays the group. The student practices holding without:

- Getting aggressive ("you guys are stupid")
- Getting apologetic ("I'm so sorry I'm such a buzzkill, never mind, fine")
- Drifting (saying nothing and just going along)

Good ways to hold ground sound like:

- "I'm going to sit this one out. Go ahead without me."
- "I don't want to. Let's do something else."
- "That's not really my thing — but you guys have fun."
- "No thanks."

Notice: you don't have to give a long explanation. "No thanks" is a complete sentence. You don't have to convince the group. You just have to hold.

Do 2–3 rounds. Gentle pushback only.

:::note Solo/Small-Group Fallback
If you don't have a partner for the role-play, the facilitator can play multiple voices of the group. If you're truly solo, write out the scene as a small script. Saying lines out loud — even to yourself — builds the same muscle.
:::

## Independent Practice

### Goal

Spot at least one alignment problem in real life this week. Notice your body's signal. Make a deliberate decision.

### Activities

**1. The Alignment Watch**

For one week, watch for alignment problems. They are everywhere once you start looking — choosing what to eat with friends, what to play at recess, what to laugh at, whether to participate in group complaining about a teacher.

When you notice one, write:

- What was the group's goal?
- What was my goal?
- Which body signals showed up?
- Did I update, hold, or drift?
- How did I feel afterward?

Even noticing one or two during the week is plenty. The skill is the noticing.

**Minimum viable version (younger learners):** Notice ONE alignment problem this week. Talk about it with the facilitator.

---

**2. The Holding Practice**

Once this week, deliberately hold ground on something — even something small. ("No thanks, I don't want to play that right now.") See what happens. See how it feels in your body. Did the group push back? Did they shift? Did anyone else hold with you?

This is genuine, real-world data. Log it.

### Telemetry Log

> Add a new section to your Telemetry Log called **Alignment Problems**:
>
> **An alignment problem I noticed this week:**
> - Group's goal: ___
> - My goal: ___
> - Body signals I noticed: ___
>
> **What I did:** updated / held / drifted
>
> **The three questions check:**
> - How much did I care about my goal? ___/10
> - Was the group's goal harmful to anyone? yes / no
> - Would I be okay with this decision tomorrow? yes / no
>
> **In hindsight, was that the right choice?** ___

**Sentence starters for younger learners:**
- "The group wanted ___ but I wanted ___."
- "I felt ___ in my body when I noticed."
- "I decided to ___."

### Reflection Questions

- Which is harder for you — updating or holding? Why?
- Have you ever drifted on something and regretted it later? What would you do differently?
- Is there a group in your life where the alignment problems happen a lot? What does that tell you?

## Check for Understanding

After this week, check whether the learner can:

1. **Define an alignment problem:** "What's an alignment problem?" (Looking for: when the group wants one thing and you want another / a goal mismatch.)
2. **Describe the three options:** "What can you do in an alignment problem?" (Looking for: update / hold / drift — and ideally that drift is the one to avoid.)
3. **Name a body signal:** "What does your body do when you're in an alignment problem?" (Looking for: any specific signal — tight stomach, fake laugh, pulled-back feeling, etc.)

If the learner can do at least 2 of these, they are ready for Week 13.

---

## Pause and Notice

:::note What Matters Here
After the role-play, ask:

> "What was it like to actually say 'no thanks' out loud? Did your body react, even though you knew it was practice?"

For most kids, even fake holding triggers a small system response. The brain reads social risk as real risk. That's old wiring. It doesn't mean you shouldn't hold — it means your brain is doing its job, treating the social moment like it matters.

The skill is *holding anyway*. Not because it's easy. Because it's important.

**This week's takeaway:** Belonging is real. Wanting to belong is not weakness. But belonging that requires you to drift is not actually belonging — it's just absorption. The friends who matter most can handle you holding ground.
:::
