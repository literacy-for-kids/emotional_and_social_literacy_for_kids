# Week 10: Interface Specs That Protect the System
*Trust, Ledgers, and Network Security*

Last week you learned to read the trust ledger. This week you learn to set the rules of the road for your own system.

When two computers talk to each other, they need an **interface specification** — an explicit description of what kinds of messages each will accept, what it will reject, and how the other side should expect it to behave. Without an interface spec, the system gets flooded with bad data and breaks.

Humans need the same thing. A **boundary** is an interface spec for a relationship — a clear statement of what you will accept, what you won't, and how you'd like to be treated. Boundaries aren't walls keeping people out. They are specifications keeping the system running.

This week you learn to set them, communicate them, and follow through.

## Facilitator Preparation

:::info Before You Begin
- Have the Telemetry Log accessible.
- Think about your own boundaries (the ones you keep AND the ones you struggle to keep). Sharing real adult examples makes this lesson land.
- For Session 2, you'll do some role-play. Be ready to play the "other side" gently — not at full intensity.
- Note: this is a vocabulary that has become very popular. Some kids will have heard "boundaries" used in oversimplified ways. Treat the engineering framing as a fresh, more precise version.
:::

:::tip Facilitation Mindset
Some kids have never been told they're allowed to have boundaries. Other kids have heard the word so much it's become hollow. Your job is to make it real:

A boundary is an actual statement that an actual person can hear and respond to. "I don't like that" is a feeling. "Please stop calling me that — I'll leave the room if it keeps happening" is a boundary.

Avoid pushing students to set boundaries they're not ready for. The goal is **understanding** the tool. Application can happen at the student's own pace.
:::

## For Younger Learners (Ages 8–9)

:::info Adapting This Week
**Simplest version of the concept:** "A boundary is the rule you teach people about how to treat you. It's not mean — it's clear."

**What to shorten or skip:**
- Skip "interface specification" terminology if it feels too abstract. Use "rules of the road" or "house rules."

**Adapting the activities:**
- Use puppets or stuffed animals for the practice — easier to set a boundary as a character.
- Use very common kid examples: "Please don't grab my markers without asking." "I don't want to be tickled when I say stop."

**Journal alternative:** "One thing I want people to do differently around me is ___. The way I might say it is ___."

**What success looks like:** The student can name at least one boundary they'd like to set with a real person — and one sentence they could use to set it.
:::

## Guided Session 1
### What an Interface Spec Looks Like
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- describe a boundary as an **interface specification** — explicit, observable, and clear
- distinguish a real boundary from a complaint, a wish, or a demand
- identify the three parts of a well-formed boundary

---

#### Activities

**1. The Computer Analogy**

Explain:

> "If a computer accepts every signal that comes in, it crashes. So computers have rules: 'I accept these kinds of messages. I reject those. If you send me garbage, I send back an error.' That's an interface spec.
>
> People need this too. Every kid has things that overload the system — being teased, being touched without permission, being talked over, being asked to do too much. Without rules, you just absorb it all until you crash."

A boundary is the human version of an interface spec.

:::note Where the Metaphor Breaks
A boundary describes what **I** will do or allow. It is not a remote control for another person.

Boundaries do not remove the need for kindness, safety, repair, or accountability. They also do not cancel safe and reasonable adult rules. A student cannot turn "boundary" into a way to avoid cleanup, school expectations, or responsibility for harm.
:::

---

**2. The Three Parts of a Boundary**

Write this on a piece of paper:

```
A WELL-FORMED BOUNDARY HAS THREE PARTS:

1. THE BEHAVIOR — What specifically you want different
2. THE REQUEST — What you want instead
3. THE FOLLOW-THROUGH — What you'll do if it keeps happening
```

Example (badly formed):
> "Stop being annoying."
> (No specific behavior. No request. No follow-through.)

Example (well-formed):
> "When you grab my stuff without asking, it bothers me. Please ask first. If you keep grabbing it, I'm going to put it in my room with the door closed."

Notice: the boundary is calm, specific, and includes a follow-through that the student can actually do.

---

**3. The Three Categories**

Boundaries usually live in three categories. Help the student think about each:

**PHYSICAL BOUNDARIES:** how people are allowed to interact with your body
- "Please don't tickle me when I say stop."
- "I don't want hugs right now."
- "Don't take my snack."

**EMOTIONAL/SOCIAL BOUNDARIES:** how people are allowed to talk to you
- "Don't call me that nickname — I don't like it."
- "I don't want to talk about my grade right now."
- "Please don't talk about my friends behind their back when I'm there."

**TIME/ATTENTION BOUNDARIES:** what you can give to others
- "I can't text after 8 PM — I'm doing my homework."
- "I'd rather play this game alone for 10 minutes — then we can play together."

Have the student name one boundary from each category they might want to set.

## Guided Session 2
### Communicating a Boundary
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- communicate a boundary out loud using all three parts
- handle a common pushback ("you're being mean" / "why are you so sensitive")
- understand that boundaries require follow-through, or they aren't real

---

#### Activities

**1. The Boundary Statement**

Take one of the student's boundaries from Session 1. Together, write it out using all three parts:

- **Behavior:** ___
- **Request:** ___
- **Follow-through:** ___

Then have the student say it out loud. It will feel awkward. That's normal. New tools always do.

---

**2. Role-Play: Pushback**

When you set a boundary, you'll sometimes get pushback. Common pushbacks include:

- "You're being too sensitive."
- "I was just joking."
- "Why are you making a big deal of this?"
- "I always do it that way."

Practice handling them. The goal isn't to win an argument — it's to **not abandon the boundary just because someone pushed back**.

A good response sounds like:

> "I'm not trying to make a big deal. I'm just letting you know — when you do ___, I don't like it. Please stop."

Or:

> "I'm not too sensitive. I just don't want ___ to happen. That's the only thing I'm asking."

Practice 2–3 rounds with the facilitator playing the pushback. Gentle pushback only at this stage.

---

**3. The Follow-Through Rule**

Here's the rule that makes boundaries actually work:

> **A boundary without follow-through is just a wish. The follow-through is what makes it real.**

If you say "I'll leave the room if it keeps happening" and then you don't leave when it keeps happening, you've taught the other person that your boundaries are negotiable.

But — and this is important — **follow-through is something YOU do.** It is not a punishment for the other person. It is an action that protects your own system. Walking away protects your system. Hanging up the phone protects your system. Telling a trusted adult protects your system.

The follow-through is yours to choose. It just has to be something you'll actually do.

## Independent Practice

### Goal

Practice formulating boundaries. Set at least one real boundary this week if possible.

### Activities

**1. The Boundary Inventory**

Make a list of 3–5 boundaries you'd like to have in your life. For each, write the three parts:

- The behavior (specific)
- The request (what you want instead)
- The follow-through (what you'll actually do)

You don't have to set them all this week. Just write them.

**Minimum viable version (younger learners):** Write one boundary using the three parts. Practice saying it out loud — even just to a stuffed animal.

---

**2. The Real-World Test (Optional)**

If there's one boundary you're ready to actually try this week, do it. Pick a small one — not a high-stakes confrontation. Examples:

- Tell a sibling "please knock before you come into my room."
- Tell a friend "I can't hang out today — I need to rest."
- Tell a parent "I'd like to finish my homework before we talk about it."

Write what happened.

If the boundary held, log the deposit it made (yes — a clear boundary can actually be a deposit in the ledger, because it makes the relationship more predictable).

If it didn't hold, note what got in the way. That's data for next time.

:::note Solo/Small-Group Fallback
The boundary inventory is solo. The role-play needs a partner — but if you don't have one, practice in front of a mirror or with a stuffed animal. Saying it out loud, even alone, builds the muscle.
:::

### Telemetry Log

> Open a new section in your Telemetry Log called **My Interface Specs**:
>
> **Boundaries I want to have:**
> 1. Behavior: ___ / Request: ___ / Follow-through: ___
> 2. Behavior: ___ / Request: ___ / Follow-through: ___
> 3. Behavior: ___ / Request: ___ / Follow-through: ___
>
> **My hardest pushback to handle:** ___ (which type of pushback knocks me off the boundary?)
>
> **A boundary I tried this week:** ___
>
> **What happened:** ___

**Sentence starters for younger learners:**
- "I'd like people to ___ instead of ___."
- "If they keep doing it, I will ___."

### Reflection Questions

- Which boundary do you wish you'd set a long time ago?
- What kind of pushback is hardest for you to handle?
- Is there a boundary someone in your life sets with you that you respect?

## Check for Understanding

After this week, check whether the learner can:

1. **Define a boundary:** "What's a boundary?" (Looking for: something about a rule for how people can treat you / an interface spec / a clear request, NOT just "saying no to people.")
2. **Name the three parts:** "What are the three parts of a well-formed boundary?" (Looking for: behavior, request, follow-through.)
3. **State a real one:** "Tell me a boundary you might set with someone in your life, including all three parts." (Looking for: a specific, well-formed statement, even if the student wouldn't actually deliver it this week.)

If the learner can do at least 2 of these, they are ready for Week 11.

## Pause and Notice

:::note What Matters Here
After working on real boundaries, ask:

> "When you imagined saying one of these boundaries out loud, what came up in your body? Tightness? Heat? A pulled-in feeling?"

For many kids — and adults — even *thinking* about setting a boundary triggers a small hijack. The system reads it as a social risk. That's normal. It doesn't mean you shouldn't set the boundary. It means your brain is using old wiring to evaluate a new tool.

Boundaries get easier with practice. Each one you set is a small deposit *into your own system* — a vote of confidence that your needs matter and you can articulate them.

**This week's takeaway:** Boundaries are not punishments. They are how a healthy system stays healthy. The clearer you can be, the easier you are to be in a relationship with.
:::
