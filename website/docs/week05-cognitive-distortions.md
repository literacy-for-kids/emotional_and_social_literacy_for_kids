# Week 5: The Software Bugs of the Mind
*Debugging the Signal Noise*

For the last four weeks, you have been learning to read the hardware — the body. This week, we move up the stack. We start looking at the **software**.

The software is the thinking part. The stories the brain spins up around emotional data. The conclusions it jumps to. The patterns it falls into.

Most of the time, the thinking software works fine. But every brain has a set of **bugs** — predictable ways the software gets things wrong. These are called **cognitive distortions**. They are not signs of a broken brain. They are signs of a normal brain running normal shortcuts.

The trick is recognizing the bugs so you can stop running buggy code as if it were the truth.

---

:::info Facilitator Snapshot
- This week is about giving names to mental patterns the student already has.
- Don't moralize. Distortions are not flaws — every human runs them. Even the facilitator.
- Be especially careful with "personalization" — kids are particularly prone to it.
- Continue the Telemetry Log. Start logging the *story* alongside the signal.
:::

## Facilitator Preparation

:::info Before You Begin
- Have the Telemetry Log accessible.
- Have sticky notes handy — they're useful for the Bug Catalog.
- Think about your own examples ahead of time. Sharing your own real cognitive distortions makes the lesson land much better than abstract examples.
- Note: the four distortions we focus on this week (catastrophizing, mind-reading, personalization, all-or-nothing) are common patterns described in cognitive-behavioral frameworks. There are many more — these four are enough to start.
:::

:::tip Facilitation Mindset
This week is hugely empowering once it clicks. Most kids walk around assuming the stories their brain tells are *true*. The realization that the brain runs predictable bugs is a genuine plot twist. Lean into that. Make it almost fun.

Avoid one trap: don't use this lesson to dismiss real concerns. If a student says "everyone hates me" and the situation is that nobody at lunch wanted to sit with them, both can be true — there's a real signal AND a bug amplifying it. The point isn't to talk them out of feeling bad. It's to debug the *interpretation* of what happened.
:::

## For Younger Learners (Ages 8–9)

:::info Adapting This Week
**Simplest version of the concept:** "Sometimes your brain tells you stories that aren't quite right — like a glitchy computer. Today we're learning what some of those glitches look like."

**What to shorten or skip:**
- Pick 2 distortions, not 4. "Catastrophizing" (making it big) and "mind-reading" (guessing what others think) are usually the most relatable.
- Skip the formal debugging steps for now — just learn to spot the bugs.

**Adapting the activities:**
- Use simple comic-strip examples. Draw a "thought bubble" with a glitchy thought, then a real situation.
- Make the bugs into cartoon characters: "The Worst-Case-Wendy" or "The Mind-Reader-Marvin." Goofy names help.

**Journal alternative:** "One time my brain told me a wrong story this week was when ___."

**What success looks like:** The student can name at least one type of brain bug and give an example from their own life — even if the example is silly.
:::

## Guided Session 1
### Naming the Bugs
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- define **cognitive distortion** in their own words
- describe four common bugs: catastrophizing, mind-reading, personalization, and all-or-nothing thinking
- give an example of each from real life

---

#### Activities

**1. The Story-Above-the-Signal**

Explain:

> "Your body sends signals. That's the hardware part — we've spent four weeks on it. But on top of every signal, your brain tells you a *story* about what the signal means. The signal might be 'tight chest.' The story your brain spins up might be 'something terrible is about to happen.' The signal is data. The story is interpretation. They are not the same thing."

Draw two columns:

| Signal (hardware) | Story (software) |
|---|---|
| Friend didn't text back | "She must be mad at me" |
| Stomach is fluttery | "I'm going to fail this test" |
| Teacher walked past without smiling | "I did something wrong" |

> "Sometimes the story is accurate. A lot of the time, the story is a buggy guess. Today we're learning to spot the most common bugs."

---

**2. The Bug Catalog**

Walk through the four common bugs with examples. Use sticky notes — one per bug — so the student can stick them up somewhere visible.

**BUG #1: Catastrophizing**
> "Taking a small thing and amplifying it into the worst possible outcome."
> Example: "I got a B on the math test. I'm going to fail the class. I'll never get into a good school. My life is ruined."

**BUG #2: Mind-Reading**
> "Assuming you know what other people are thinking — usually that they're thinking something negative about you."
> Example: "She looked at me weird at recess. She's definitely mad at me." (You don't actually know what she was thinking — she might have had something in her eye.)

**BUG #3: Personalization**
> "Assuming things are about you when they're often not."
> Example: "Dad came home in a bad mood. I must have done something wrong." (Dad had a bad day at work. It wasn't about you at all.)

**BUG #4: All-or-Nothing Thinking**
> "Seeing things as totally good or totally bad, with no middle. Often involves the words 'always' or 'never.'"
> Example: "I made one mistake in the game. I'm the WORST. I always mess up. I'll never be good at this."

---

**3. Wrap-Up: The Most Important Rule**

Write this on the wall, or in big letters in the log:

> **A thought is not a fact. Just because your brain says it doesn't make it true.**

This is the foundational rule of this entire unit. Read it out loud. Have the student read it out loud.

## Guided Session 2
### Catching Your Own Bugs
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- identify their own most common cognitive distortion
- catch a distortion in a real moment (even after the fact counts)
- begin the practice of debugging — asking "is this thought a fact?"

---

#### Activities

**1. Your Most-Run Bug**

Look at the four bugs from Session 1. Ask:

> "Which one do you think YOU run the most? Be honest. There's no wrong answer."

Most kids will know right away. The mind-reader. The catastrophizer. The personalizer. The all-or-nothing thinker. Almost everyone runs at least one of these heavily.

Add it to the Telemetry Log: **"My most common cognitive distortion is ___."**

This isn't a label that defines them. It's just a heads-up for what to watch for.

---

**2. The "Is This a Fact?" Test**

Introduce a simple two-step debugging move:

1. **Notice the thought.** ("I think she's mad at me.")
2. **Ask: Is this a fact?** ("Did she actually say she's mad? Did anything she did directly tell me that? Or is my brain filling in a story?")

Almost every cognitive distortion fails the "is this a fact?" test. The thought is a guess dressed up as a fact.

Try it with a recent example from the student's life:

- What did your brain say?
- What's the actual evidence?
- What's another possible explanation?

There is usually more than one possible story. The bug is when the brain picks the worst one and treats it as the only one.

---

**3. The Alternative Story Challenge**

For one of the student's recent worried thoughts, brainstorm 3 alternative explanations.

Example thought: "My friend didn't sit with me at lunch. She must not like me anymore."

Alternative stories:

- She had to ask a teacher a question and arrived late
- She got pulled into another conversation and didn't see me
- She thought I was sitting somewhere else
- She was upset about her own stuff and needed space
- She just felt like sitting somewhere else today — no big deal

Note: the original story might still be true. The point of the alternatives isn't to prove the brain wrong — it's to break the spell where one story feels like the only possible one.

## Independent Practice

### Goal

Catch at least three cognitive distortions in your own thinking this week. Name them.

### Activities

**1. The Bug Hunt**

For one week, watch for thoughts that look like the four bugs. When you notice one, write down:

- The thought
- Which bug it is (catastrophizing / mind-reading / personalization / all-or-nothing)
- The "is this a fact?" check
- One alternative story

You don't have to make the thought go away. You just have to **name it**.

**Minimum viable version (younger learners):** Catch ONE buggy thought this week. Name which bug it is. Tell the facilitator.

---

**2. The Top Hits Tracker**

By the end of the week, count up your bug catches. Which one did you run the most?

That's your **top hit** — the bug to watch most carefully going forward.

:::note Solo/Small-Group Fallback
All bug-hunting activities are individual. No partner needed.
:::

### Telemetry Log

> Add a new section to your Telemetry Log this week:
>
> **My Top Cognitive Distortion:** ___
>
> **Bugs I caught this week:**
> 1. Thought: ___ / Bug type: ___ / Was it a fact? ___
> 2. Thought: ___ / Bug type: ___ / Was it a fact? ___
> 3. Thought: ___ / Bug type: ___ / Was it a fact? ___
>
> **The story I'd tell instead:** ___

**Sentence starters for younger learners:**
- "My brain told me ___, but actually ___."
- "The bug I run the most is ___."

### Reflection Questions

- Which bug was easiest to spot in yourself?
- Did catching the bug change how you felt? Or did the feeling stay even when you knew the thought was buggy?
- Have you noticed any of these bugs in the people around you (parents, friends, characters in shows)?

## Check for Understanding

After this week, check whether the learner can:

1. **Define cognitive distortion:** "What's a cognitive distortion, in your own words?" (Looking for: something like a thinking bug / a buggy thought / a story your brain tells that isn't quite true.)
2. **Name at least two bugs:** "Tell me about two of the brain bugs we learned." (Looking for: any two — catastrophizing, mind-reading, personalization, all-or-nothing — with rough definitions.)
3. **Apply the test:** "If you have a worried thought, what's one question you can ask yourself?" (Looking for: "is this a fact?" or "what's the evidence?" or "could there be another reason?")

If the learner can do at least 2 of these, they are ready for Week 6.

---

## Pause and Notice

:::note What Matters Here
After the bug hunt, ask:

> "When you caught a buggy thought this week and named it, what happened to the feeling? Did it shrink? Did it stay? Did it change shape?"

This is one of the most useful skills in the whole course. Sometimes naming a bug shrinks the feeling. Sometimes it doesn't — and that's okay. Even if the feeling stays, you've separated the *interpretation* from the *signal*. You now know the feeling exists for some reason, but the worst-case story your brain spun up isn't necessarily true.

A lot of mental work is about getting that small gap between *signal* and *story*. That gap is where freedom lives.

**This week's takeaway:** Your brain runs buggy code sometimes. That's normal. Debugging is a skill anyone can build — and you just started.
:::
