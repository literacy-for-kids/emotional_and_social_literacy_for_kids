# Emotional & Social Literacy for Kids

An 18-week, adult-facilitated curriculum for ages 8-12 that treats emotions as telemetry and social life as a system kids can learn to read, debug, and improve.

Most social and emotional education focuses on compliance, vague self-expression, or rules without models. This curriculum takes a different approach: students learn that emotions are signals, reactivity is a state change, trust is built through repeated behavior, and conflict is often a solvable system mismatch rather than a moral verdict.

This is an educational curriculum, not therapy. Students may use fictional or low-stakes examples, and adults are expected to protect privacy, choice, and safety while teaching the tools.

## At a Glance

- 18 weeks, usually taught in three short sessions per week
- About 20-30 minutes per session
- Designed for classrooms, homeschool, caregivers, co-ops, and after-school settings
- Includes weekly lessons, facilitator guides, student printables, assessment guidance, and a competency map

### Core Concepts

These mental models help students move from reactivity toward observation, reasoning, and deliberate action.

- **Emotions are Telemetry, Not Commands:** Feelings and body signals carry information. Students practice reading the data without treating the first impulse as an order.
- **Reactivity Is a State Change:** Under stress, the brain shifts into fast protective mode. Naming the shift helps students pause before they act.
- **Trust Behaves Like a Ledger:** Reliability, honesty, repair, and follow-through build trust over time. Broken agreements and inconsistency drain it.
- **Conflict Is Often a System Mismatch:** Friction is commonly caused by mismatched goals, hidden constraints, or bad information rather than simple badness.
- **Communication Creates Ripple Effects:** Messages travel through relationships and groups. Students learn to think about impact, not just intent.

### The Curriculum Roadmap

This 18-week progression moves from understanding the self to navigating relationships, groups, and a final low-stakes protocol-design capstone.

### Weeks 1–4: Internal Telemetry & Hardware States

**Focus:** Sensory inputs, processing, and the nervous system
Students map their own hardware, notice how sleep, hunger, noise, and overload affect processing capacity, and build a Telemetry Log for pattern noticing.

- **Key tools:** Telemetry Log, capacity tracking, early-warning sign noticing

### Weeks 5–8: Debugging the Signal Noise

**Focus:** Cognitive distortions, internal narratives, and filtering
Students learn to separate what happened from the story the brain added, then test more accurate and useful interpretations.

- **Key tools:** Thought Bug Debugger, Input/Output Audit, Feedback Loop Map

### Weeks 9–11: Trust, Ledgers, and Network Security

**Focus:** Boundary setting, reliability, and social capital
Students explore how trust builds, how repair works, and how boundaries protect a system without becoming tools for control.

- **Key tools:** Trust Repair Planner, Boundary Script Builder

### Weeks 12–14: Game Theory in Groups

**Focus:** Social dynamics, cooperation, and asymmetric information
Students examine peer pressure, rumors, group incentives, and hidden variables, including modern digital-life examples such as screenshots, group chats, and forwarding choices.

- **Key tools:** Rumor Stop-Node Checklist, conflict diagnostics, group-pattern analysis

### Weeks 15–18: The Social Interface Patch

**Focus:** Conflict resolution and system optimization
Students choose a manageable, facilitator-approved friction point in their own behavior, communication, or environment, then design, test, and reflect on a personal protocol.

- **Key tools:** Social System 5 Whys, Personal Protocol Template, Seven-Day Deployment Tracker, Patch Notes Reflection

### Optional Extension Topics

- **Optional Week 1:** Advanced regulation tools such as box breathing, somatic grounding, and progressive muscle relaxation, taught with clear opt-out and substitution language
- **Optional Week 2:** Complex group dynamics and social mapping, taught with strict privacy rules and fictional or anonymized examples
